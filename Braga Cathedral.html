Braga Cathedral

Braga Cathedral (Portuguese: Sé de Braga) is one of Portugal’s most historically significant churches, located in the city of Braga. Consecrated in the 11th century, it is the oldest cathedral in Portugal and a central monument in the country’s Christian heritage.

Historical background

The cathedral was commissioned soon after the Christian reconquest of Braga from Moorish rule, symbolizing the city’s renewed ecclesiastical prominence. Its construction began under Bishop Pedro and continued through the 12th century, with successive expansions that mirror Portugal’s early medieval growth. Braga later became the seat of the Primate of All Portugal, underscoring the cathedral’s national importance.

Architecture and art

Braga Cathedral showcases an architectural palimpsest: Romanesque foundations with later Gothic chapels, Manueline ornamentation, and Baroque embellishments. The twin towers of its façade—rebuilt in the 18th century—frame an elaborately sculpted portal. Inside, the gilded choir stalls, ornate organs, and the Capela dos Reis (Chapel of the Kings) exemplify Portugal’s evolving religious art from the 14th to 18th centuries.

Cultural and religious significance

Beyond its architectural richness, the cathedral has long been a center of Portuguese spiritual life. It houses the tombs of Portugal’s first royal ancestors and maintains a treasury with vestments, reliquaries, and sacred art spanning centuries. The site remains a focal point for major religious ceremonies and a prominent stop for visitors exploring Portugal’s oldest diocese.
