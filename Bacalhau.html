Bacalhau

Bacalhau is the Portuguese term for dried and salted codfish, a centuries-old ingredient that has become the country’s most emblematic food. Though the cod itself comes from northern waters such as Norway or Iceland, its preserved form is central to Portuguese identity and cuisine, appearing in both humble taverns and festive holiday feasts.

Origins and preservation

Bacalhau’s importance dates to the Age of Discovery, when Portuguese sailors needed durable protein for long voyages. Salting and air-drying cod from the North Atlantic kept it edible for months, enabling maritime expansion and trade. Religion also boosted its popularity: Catholic fasting days forbade meat, making salted cod a practical, affordable substitute.

Culinary role

Today, bacalhau remains the cornerstone of Portuguese cooking. It is soaked for up to 72 hours to remove excess salt, then baked, boiled, grilled, or fried in myriad forms. Signature dishes include Bacalhau à Brás (shredded cod with eggs, onions, and fried potatoes), Bacalhau com Natas (cod baked with cream), Bacalhau à Lagareiro (grilled with garlic and olive oil), and Pastéis de Bacalhau (fried cod fritters). Regional variations abound, from the Azores to Alentejo.

Cultural significance

More than a food, bacalhau symbolizes resilience and shared heritage. It bridges Portugal’s seafaring past and present-day table, uniting coastal and inland communities. The saying “there is a bacalhau recipe for every day of the year” captures its enduring versatility and affection in Portuguese life.
