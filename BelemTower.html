Belém Tower

Belém Tower (Portuguese: Torre de Belém) is a fortified tower on the northern bank of the Tagus River in Lisbon, Portugal. Built in the early 16th century, it is a prominent example of Manueline architecture and a UNESCO World Heritage Site, symbolizing Portugal’s Age of Discovery.

Historical background

Commissioned by King Manuel I of Portugal, Belém Tower was built to guard the entrance to the Tagus River and protect Lisbon’s harbor. It was also a ceremonial gateway to the city for explorers returning from overseas expeditions. The tower once stood in midstream but is now near the shore due to river course changes.

Architectural features

The structure combines a four-story tower with a bastion extending into the river. Its design features intricate stone carvings, including maritime motifs, armillary spheres, and the cross of the Order of Christ. Distinctive elements such as bartizans, decorative shields, and rhinoceros-shaped gargoyles demonstrate Portugal’s architectural innovation during the Renaissance.

Cultural significance

Belém Tower is regarded as a symbol of Portugal’s maritime heritage and exploration era, closely linked with figures such as Vasco da Gama. It appears on numerous Portuguese coins and stamps and remains one of Lisbon’s most visited landmarks, attracting millions of tourists annually.

Preservation and tourism

Recognized by UNESCO for its historical and architectural importance, the tower is maintained by Direção-Geral do Património Cultural (Portugal). Visitors can explore its terraces, dungeons, and panoramic viewpoints overlooking the Tagus River and the surrounding Belém district.
