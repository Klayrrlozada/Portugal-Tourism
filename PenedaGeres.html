Peneda-Gerês National Park

Peneda-Gerês National Park is Portugal’s only national park, located in the country’s mountainous northwest along the border with Galicia, Spain. Covering about 70,000 hectares, it is renowned for its rugged landscapes, ancient forests, and well-preserved rural villages that blend human life harmoniously with nature.

Landscape and ecology

The park unites four granite mountain ranges—Peneda, Soajo, Amarela, and Gerês—carved by glacial valleys and crossed by the Lima and Cávado Rivers. Its habitats range from oak forests and peat bogs to alpine meadows, sheltering more than 800 plant and 1,200 animal species. Notable wildlife includes the Iberian wolf, roe deer (the park’s symbol), and the garrano, a small semi-wild pony. The Mata da Albergaria forest and numerous waterfalls, such as Arado and Tahiti (Fecha de Barjas), highlight the park’s pristine ecology.

Cultural heritage

Peneda-Gerês is also a living cultural landscape. Dozens of stone villages—Soajo, Lindoso, and Castro Laboreiro—preserve communal granaries (espigueiros), medieval castles, and Romanesque churches. The 2,000-year-old Roman Geira road still winds through the forest. Seasonal migration between “brandas” (summer pastures) and “inverneiras” (winter hamlets) continues to reflect ancestral mountain traditions.

Activities and access

Hiking, canyoning, kayaking, horseback riding, and wildlife watching are popular, while viewpoints like Pedra Bela offer sweeping panoramas. The park can be reached in about 1½ hours by car from Porto or less than an hour from Braga. Visitor centers, known as Portas do Parque, operate in Lamas de Mouro, Mezio, Lindoso, Campo do Gerês, and Montalegre, providing maps and trail information.

Conservation and significance

Recognized by the Council of Europe as a Biogenetic Reserve, Peneda-Gerês balances environmental protection with sustainable rural life. Its designation as part of the Gerês-Xurés Transboundary Biosphere Reserve underscores cooperation between Portugal and Spain in preserving one of the Iberian Peninsula’s most biodiverse and culturally rich regions.
